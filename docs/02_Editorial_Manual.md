# Editorial Manual

## Purpose

Define the editorial standards for Eco Tiny Living Hub (ETLH) so every article, social post, caption, resource, recommendation, and supporting message protects reader trust and reflects the approved brand foundation.

## Scope

This manual covers:

- Editorial voice and tone
- Reader treatment
- Writing standards
- Content structure
- Claims and evidence standards
- Product and affiliate language
- Platform adaptation
- Accessibility and clarity
- Editorial review and quality control

Research collection and evidence-record procedures belong in `docs/03_Research_Manual.md`. Visual execution belongs in `docs/04_Design_System.md`. Operational publishing steps belong in `docs/05_Publishing_Playbook.md`.

## Status

Canonical editorial standard v1.0.

This document consolidates approved ETLH principles and existing published-content practices. It does not replace the Brand Foundation or create a new brand position.

## Editorial North Star

Every ETLH piece should help the reader feel lighter, clearer, or more capable.

The work should leave the reader with more value than they arrived with and make one useful next step easier to see or take.

## Reader-Centered Standard

The reader is the center of the work. The creator is the guide, not the hero.

Write to help the reader:

- Understand a real problem
- See why it keeps repeating
- Reduce unnecessary friction
- Choose one practical action
- Build a system that fits real life
- Feel capable without being pressured

Do not make the piece primarily about the creator's identity, lifestyle, taste, discipline, or transformation unless that personal detail directly serves the reader.

## Voice

ETLH voice is:

- Calm
- Practical
- Trustworthy
- Clear
- Warm
- Respectful
- Grounded
- Encouraging without hype
- Confident without pretending certainty
- Useful before inspirational

ETLH should sound like a thoughtful guide helping someone make life work a little better—not a guru, lecturer, salesperson, or perfectionist.

## Tone

Tone should adapt to the reader's likely state while remaining recognizably ETLH.

### When the reader feels overwhelmed

Use a low-pressure tone. Narrow the task. Emphasize that one step is enough.

### When the reader feels frustrated

Acknowledge the repeated friction without blaming the reader. Shift attention from personal failure to the system, placement, sequence, or constraint.

### When teaching

Be direct, specific, and concrete. Explain why the action helps and what limitation it does not solve.

### When recommending a product

Be restrained, transparent, and value-focused. Explain who it may help, who may not need it, and whether a free or lower-cost alternative exists.

### When discussing sustainability

Teach, do not preach. Avoid moral ranking, purity language, and guilt.

## Core Editorial Principles

### Trust Is the Main Currency

Protect long-term trust over attention, output volume, affiliate revenue, or short-term engagement.

Do not:

- Exaggerate outcomes
- Manufacture urgency
- Invent evidence
- Present assumptions as facts
- Hide meaningful limitations
- Recommend weak products for monetization
- Imply that buying is required for progress
- Use unsupported savings, health, environmental, or performance claims

### Progress Matters More Than Perfection

Favor realistic improvement over idealized transformation.

Show readers what can be done within limited space, time, money, energy, and rental restrictions.

### Teach, Don't Preach

Explain the practical reason behind an action. Preserve the reader's agency.

Avoid language that tells readers they are careless, wasteful, irresponsible, lazy, or morally behind.

### Focused Consistency Creates Lasting Change

Prefer one repeatable action over a long list that creates diffused attention.

Lists are useful only when they remain navigable and help the reader choose. Do not confuse quantity with value.

### Reduce Unnecessary Friction

Every piece should identify or reduce at least one unnecessary demand on attention, time, energy, money, or space.

## Reader Treatment

Assume the reader is capable and operating within real constraints.

Respect:

- Small budgets
- Rental limitations
- Limited storage
- Shared homes
- Disability and varying energy levels
- Time pressure
- Different household structures
- Different levels of sustainability knowledge
- The right to decide that a recommendation is not useful

Never treat the reader's home as a moral reflection of their worth.

Avoid implying that clutter, waste, unfinished tasks, or inconsistent habits prove a character flaw.

## Writing Standards

### Clarity First

Use plain language unless a technical term is necessary. Define unfamiliar terms when first used.

Prefer:

- Short, direct sentences
- Concrete nouns and verbs
- Specific examples
- Clear sequence
- Useful headings
- Scannable paragraphs

Avoid:

- Inflated language
- Empty motivation
- Corporate filler
- Trend jargon without meaning
- Overlong introductions
- Repeating the same point in several forms
- Clever wording that reduces clarity

### Show, Don't Merely Assert

Do not say a method is easy, affordable, sustainable, calming, or effective without showing why.

Instead of:

> This simple organizer will transform your space.

Use:

> A shallow tray near the door can keep keys and mail from spreading across several surfaces.

### Be Specific About the Action

A practical ETLH action should answer:

- What should the reader do?
- Where should they do it?
- What friction does it reduce?
- What does it cost?
- How long might it take?
- What constraint could affect it?
- What is the smallest useful version?

Not every piece needs every answer, but the reader should not be left with a vague instruction such as “be more intentional.”

### Use Modest Language

Prefer:

- can
- may
- often
- is designed to
- can help
- is useful when
- one option is

Use absolute language only when it is genuinely supportable.

Avoid:

- always
- never
- guaranteed
- life-changing
- perfect
- effortless
- zero impact
- completely sustainable
- saves everyone money

## ETLH Terminology

### Eco Step

An Eco Step is a small, practical, repeatable action that reduces friction, supports the reader's values, or makes a helpful habit easier to repeat.

Use the term only when the action is concrete enough to take.

### Friction

Friction is an unnecessary demand that repeatedly consumes attention, energy, time, money, or effort without meaningfully supporting what matters.

Do not use “friction” as a vague synonym for every inconvenience. Identify the repeated demand.

### Focused Consistency

Focused consistency means taking manageable actions in a coherent direction rather than scattering attention across too many changes.

### Thoughtful Living

Thoughtful living means directing effort toward what meaningfully supports the reader's values, home, goals, and well-being.

## Content Structure

A strong ETLH piece usually contains:

1. A recognizable reader problem or desired outcome
2. A clear explanation of the friction or constraint
3. One practical action or small system
4. Examples that fit small-space life
5. Relevant limitations or alternatives
6. A low-pressure next step

This is a flexible structure, not a rigid template.

## Openings

Begin close to the reader's actual problem.

Useful opening approaches include:

- Name a repeated frustration
- Ask a focused question
- Describe a familiar small-space moment
- Correct an unhelpful assumption
- Offer a narrow starting point

Avoid beginning with broad statements about the planet, generic lifestyle philosophy, or lengthy creator backstory when the reader came for practical help.

## Headlines and Titles

A title should accurately describe the value of the piece.

Good titles are:

- Specific
- Reader-centered
- Search-appropriate when used for the website
- Honest about scope
- Free of unsupported promises

Do not use clickbait that the content cannot satisfy.

Numbers may be used when the numbered structure genuinely improves usefulness. Do not inflate lists to reach a marketable number.

## Calls to Action

Calls to action should reduce pressure and point to a useful next step.

Appropriate calls to action include:

- Choose one surface
- Save this for your next reset
- Try the smallest version today
- Notice what keeps repeating
- Pick one Eco Step
- Read the related guide
- Download a relevant resource

Do not create false urgency or imply that engagement proves commitment.

Avoid manipulative prompts such as:

- Comment if you truly care
- Buy before it is too late
- You have no excuse
- Real sustainable people do this

## Evidence and Claims Standards

### Separate Evidence, Interpretation, and Advice

A piece may contain:

- Evidence: what a reliable source or real person reported
- Interpretation: what ETLH reasonably concludes from that evidence
- Advice: what the reader may try

Do not blur these categories.

### Evidence Threshold

Use evidence that is relevant to the exact claim. The strength of the wording must not exceed the strength of the evidence.

Anecdotes may illustrate an experience but do not establish prevalence, causation, or universal effectiveness.

### Quotes

Keep quotes verbatim except for clearly marked, minimal corrections that do not change meaning. Do not manufacture composite quotes.

Preserve source context so the quote is not made to support a claim the speaker did not make.

### Statistics

Before publishing a statistic, record:

- Original source
- Publication date
- Population or sample
- Geographic scope
- What was measured
- Relevant limitation

Do not cite a secondary article when the primary report is reasonably available.

### Sustainability Claims

Be precise about the boundary of the claim.

Avoid broad claims such as “eco-friendly,” “zero waste,” “non-toxic,” or “sustainable” without explaining the relevant attribute.

Prefer specific wording such as:

- reusable instead of disposable for this use
- made with recycled material
- designed to reduce food waste
- may reduce electricity use compared with the stated alternative

Do not imply that one product or habit has no environmental tradeoffs.

### Savings Claims

Do not claim a step saves money unless the mechanism and relevant assumptions are clear.

Distinguish:

- Lower upfront cost
- Lower recurring cost
- Avoided purchase
- Possible utility savings
- Longer useful life

Do not calculate savings using unrealistic behavior or undisclosed prices.

### Health and Safety Claims

Health, mold, air-quality, food-safety, chemical, electrical, and structural claims require higher scrutiny.

Do not diagnose, guarantee safety, or substitute lifestyle content for qualified professional guidance.

State when local rules, product instructions, a landlord, or a qualified professional should be consulted.

## Product and Affiliate Editorial Standards

A recommendation must have clear reader value independent of commission.

Before recommending a product, ask:

- What problem does it solve?
- Is buying necessary?
- Is there a free or lower-cost alternative?
- Does it fit a genuinely small space?
- Is it renter-friendly where relevant?
- What are its limitations?
- Is the claim supported?
- Would ETLH still recommend it without compensation?

Disclose affiliate relationships clearly and near the recommendation when affiliate publishing begins.

Never:

- Hide sponsorship
- Create fake scarcity
- Invent reviews
- Use fake ratings or badges
- Present the most expensive choice as automatically best
- Recommend duplicate products that increase clutter without meaningful benefit

## Platform Standards

### Website Articles

Website articles should:

- Satisfy the title and search intent
- Include a clear introduction and useful headings
- Address small-space constraints
- Provide one or more concrete actions
- Include internal links only when genuinely relevant
- Use accurate publication and update dates
- Include complete metadata and accessible image descriptions
- Avoid padding for word count

### Instagram Carousels

Use the locked workflow:

1. Visual Batch Prompt
2. Copy Template
3. Caption + Hashtags in one copy box
4. First Comment
5. Alt Text
6. Codex Backlog Addition when needed

Carousel copy should:

- Make one primary point
- Keep each slide readable on a phone
- Use concise headlines and limited body copy
- Build a clear sequence
- End with one practical Eco Step or low-pressure action when appropriate

Every final carousel package requires caption, hashtags, first comment, and alt text.

### Pinterest

Pinterest packaging should be search-aware and visually scannable.

Pin titles and descriptions must accurately match the linked content. Do not use a high-intent promise that the destination does not fulfill.

Graphics should preserve the text-safe and brand standards in the Design System.

### Email and Downloads

Email and downloadable resources should deliver the promised value directly. Do not use a lead magnet title that overstates what the resource contains.

Keep instructions practical and easy to resume after interruption.

## Accessibility and Inclusive Clarity

### Alt Text

Describe the meaningful visual information and the purpose of the image in context.

For text-based carousel slides, include the important overlaid message in the alt text without reproducing unnecessary decorative detail.

Do not keyword-stuff alt text.

### Readability

Use sufficient contrast, readable type, clear hierarchy, and manageable paragraph length.

Do not rely on color alone to communicate meaning.

### Inclusive Language

Avoid assumptions about:

- Income
- Physical ability
- Home ownership
- Family structure
- Gender roles
- Access to outdoor space
- Access to specialty stores
- Time and energy

Offer alternatives where a recommendation depends on those conditions.

## Prohibited Editorial Approaches

ETLH should not publish content that relies on:

- Shame or guilt
- Sustainability purity tests
- Fear without practical context
- Unsupported certainty
- False urgency
- Fake personal stories
- Invented evidence
- Misleading before-and-after framing
- Hidden advertising
- Product-first content with no clear reader problem
- Creator-centered self-promotion disguised as education
- Generic inspiration with no usable next step
- Overpromising transformation
- Treating a purchase as the only solution

## Editorial Review Checklist

Before approval, confirm:

### Reader Value

- Does this address a real reader problem, constraint, or desired outcome?
- Will the reader leave lighter, clearer, or more capable?
- Is there a practical next step?

### Brand Alignment

- Does it reduce friction, waste, overwhelm, or unnecessary effort?
- Does it respect progress over perfection?
- Does it teach rather than preach?
- Is the reader more important than the messenger?

### Accuracy

- Are factual claims supported?
- Are assumptions labeled?
- Are dates, prices, measurements, and links accurate?
- Are limitations included where they matter?
- Is the wording no stronger than the evidence?

### Clarity

- Is the main point obvious?
- Can unnecessary text be removed?
- Are examples concrete?
- Is terminology defined?

### Trust

- Is any commercial relationship disclosed?
- Would ETLH stand behind the recommendation without compensation?
- Is anything likely to mislead a reasonable reader?

### Accessibility

- Is alt text complete where required?
- Is the copy readable on the intended platform?
- Does the language avoid unnecessary exclusion?

### Production Completeness

- Are all required platform components present?
- Does the title match the content?
- Do links lead to the promised destination?
- Does the recorded status match actual production state?

## Corrections Standard

When an error is found:

1. Verify the error.
2. Correct the source of truth, not only the visible symptom.
3. Update affected metadata or derivative assets when relevant.
4. Record material corrections in the appropriate project log.
5. Do not hide the correction when the original error could meaningfully affect trust.
