# Research Manual

## Purpose

Define how Eco Tiny Living Hub (ETLH) collects, evaluates, records, synthesizes, and applies evidence so content decisions remain grounded in real reader problems rather than assumptions, trend chasing, or convenient confirmation.

## Scope

This manual covers:

- Research questions and hypotheses
- Source selection
- First-hand evidence collection
- Quote handling
- Evidence-record structure
- Classification of problems, emotions, constraints, jobs-to-be-done, and attempted solutions
- Evidence-strength assessment
- Exclusion and duplication rules
- Theme synthesis
- Claim support
- Product and market research safeguards
- Research handoff into editorial and publishing work

Editorial wording and claims standards belong in `docs/02_Editorial_Manual.md`. Visual production belongs in `docs/04_Design_System.md`. Publishing operations belong in `docs/05_Publishing_Playbook.md`.

## Status

Canonical research standard v1.0.

This document consolidates the established ETLH evidence-review workflow and approved research principles. It does not create new strategy or replace source records.

## Research North Star

Research exists to understand what real people are experiencing, trying to accomplish, and struggling against.

The goal is not to prove an ETLH assumption correct. The goal is to identify what the available evidence actually supports, what it does not support, and where uncertainty remains.

## Core Research Principles

### Do Not Invent Evidence

Never fabricate:

- Quotes
- Comments
- Reviews
- Statistics
- Source details
- Problems
- Emotions
- Failed solutions
- Audience demand
- Product interest
- Market size

When evidence is missing, say so.

### Do Not Confirm Assumptions by Default

A hypothesis is a question to test, not a conclusion to defend.

Research should be willing to conclude that:

- The theme is weak
- The problem is uncommon
- The evidence is too generic
- The source is promotional
- The proposed category is wrong
- The emotional interpretation is overstated
- The audience is different from the one expected
- A solution already works well enough
- No strong content opportunity is visible

### Separate Observation from Interpretation

Keep distinct:

- What the source literally said
- What context surrounds it
- What ETLH infers from it
- How confident that inference is
- What content or product decision may follow

Do not rewrite an inference as though the source stated it directly.

### Trust Is the Main Currency

Research quality is part of reader trust.

It is better to publish a smaller, qualified conclusion than a broad conclusion built on weak evidence.

### Reader Reality Comes Before Content Convenience

Do not force evidence into a preselected content idea, keyword, product, or affiliate opportunity.

Content should follow the supported problem, constraint, and job-to-be-done.

## Research Questions

A useful research question is narrow enough to test and specific enough to guide collection.

A research question should identify, where relevant:

- Audience
- Situation
- Problem or desired outcome
- Environment or constraint
- Timeframe
- Type of evidence needed

Example:

> What repeated storage, clutter, and decision problems do renters in small apartments describe when trying to maintain low-waste routines?

Avoid questions that assume the answer, such as:

> Why do small-apartment renters need our organization guide?

## Hypothesis Handling

Record a hypothesis before reviewing the evidence when possible.

A useful hypothesis record includes:

- Hypothesis statement
- Audience
- Expected problem
- Expected emotion
- Expected constraint
- What evidence would support it
- What evidence would weaken or disprove it
- Collection boundaries

After review, classify the hypothesis as:

- Supported
- Partially supported
- Weakly supported
- Not supported
- Contradicted
- Inconclusive

Do not change the original hypothesis after seeing the evidence. Record the conclusion separately.

## Preferred Source Types

ETLH research has an established preference for first-hand or near-first-hand evidence where people describe their own experiences.

Potential sources include:

- Reddit posts and comments
- YouTube comments
- Quora answers and discussions
- Public Facebook-group discussions where access and use are appropriate
- Amazon and retailer reviews
- Pinterest comments
- Blog and forum comments
- Community forums
- Public interviews
- Reader emails or survey responses when permission and privacy requirements are met
- News reporting that includes direct accounts

Secondary sources may be used for context, statistics, regulation, technical facts, or expert guidance, but they should not be mistaken for direct audience evidence.

## Source Selection Standards

Prefer sources that are:

- Relevant to the exact audience and situation
- Specific about the problem or desired outcome
- Detailed enough to interpret responsibly
- Independent of the product or claim being evaluated
- Recent enough for the question
- Traceable to an original page or record
- Context-rich rather than isolated fragments

Use caution with sources that are:

- Promotional
- Sponsored without clear disclosure
- Affiliate-driven
- Reposted without source context
- Written by bots or content farms
- Extremely generic
- Repeating a common phrase without personal detail
- Incentivized reviews
- Duplicated across platforms
- Based on hearsay

## Collection Boundaries

Before collecting evidence, define:

- Included audiences
- Excluded audiences
- Included locations or housing types
- Included problem categories
- Included time range
- Language limitations
- Source types
- Minimum context required
- Maximum number of records
- Stopping condition

A stopping condition may be reached when:

- New records mostly repeat existing themes
- The target number of strong records is reached
- The source pool is exhausted
- Additional collection no longer changes the conclusion

Do not claim statistical saturation unless the method supports that claim. “Themes became repetitive in this sample” is safer.

## Quote Collection Standard

Capture the source's wording exactly whenever a quote is used as evidence.

For each quote, preserve:

- Exact quote text
- Source platform
- Source URL or durable locator
- Author or username when ethically appropriate
- Publication or retrieval date
- Thread, review, video, product, or article context
- Relevant surrounding text
- Whether the quote was edited or excerpted

Do not:

- Correct grammar silently when meaning could change
- Merge multiple people into one quote
- Present a paraphrase as a quote
- Remove context that changes the meaning
- Translate without recording that a translation occurred

## Privacy and Ethical Handling

Public availability does not remove the need for care.

Avoid collecting or reproducing unnecessary personal information.

When publishing audience evidence:

- Use the minimum identifying detail needed
- Avoid exposing sensitive information
- Avoid directing attention toward a private individual
- Consider paraphrasing when the exact wording is not essential
- Follow platform rules and applicable privacy requirements
- Obtain permission for private emails, surveys, or direct messages when needed

Do not use vulnerable disclosures as content bait.

## Evidence Record Structure

Each evidence record should contain, when available:

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Record ID | Unique stable identifier |
| Lane | Research lane or broad topic |
| Source Type | Reddit, review, comment, interview, report, and so on |
| Source Locator | URL or durable source reference |
| Retrieval Date | Date collected |
| Exact Quote | Verbatim evidence text |
| Context | Relevant surrounding information |
| Audience | Who appears to be speaking |
| Housing or Situation | Apartment, studio, renter, tiny home, and so on |
| Problem Category | Best-supported problem classification |
| Dominant Emotion | Primary emotional signal supported by the wording |
| Job-to-Be-Done | What the person is trying to accomplish |
| Constraints | Conditions making the job harder |
| Existing or Attempted Solution | What they tried or currently use |
| Outcome | Whether the attempted solution worked |
| Evidence Strength | Strong, moderate, weak, or excluded |
| Exclusion Flags | Generic, promotional, duplicate, unsupported, unclear, or other |
| Analyst Notes | Clearly labeled interpretation and uncertainty |

Do not leave a classification unchallenged merely because it appeared in the original data.

## Problem Classification

Classify the problem described, not the content idea ETLH hopes to create.

Established ETLH research categories have included:

- Storage
- Organization
- Visual clutter
- Laundry
- Humidity
- Mold
- Rental restrictions
- Old appliances
- Budget
- High cost
- Time pressure
- Composting
- Waste

Add a new category only when existing categories do not fit and multiple records justify the distinction.

When a quote contains several problems, identify:

- Primary problem
- Secondary problem or problems
- Relationship between them

Do not count the same quote as independent proof of several themes without noting that it is one record.

## Emotional Classification

Established ETLH emotional signals have included:

- Frustration
- Overwhelm
- Resignation
- Anxiety
- Hope

Classify the emotion supported by the language, not the emotion expected from the topic.

Distinguish emotion from situation.

For example:

- “I have nowhere to put this” describes a constraint or problem.
- “I feel defeated every time I clean” supports resignation or frustration.

When the emotional signal is unclear, record “unclear” rather than guessing.

## Job-to-Be-Done Extraction

The job-to-be-done describes the progress the person is trying to make in a particular situation.

Write it in practical language, such as:

> Keep food scraps out of the trash without creating smell or taking up scarce counter space.

A useful job-to-be-done should identify:

- Desired progress
- Situation
- Important constraint or tradeoff

Avoid vague statements such as:

> Be more sustainable.

Do not confuse a product with the job. “Buy a countertop bin” is a solution. The job may be “store food scraps cleanly in a small kitchen until disposal.”

## Constraint Extraction

Record the conditions limiting available solutions.

Common ETLH constraints include:

- Limited square footage
- Limited counter space
- No outdoor space
- Rental rules
- Shared household
- Small budget
- High local costs
- Limited time
- Low energy
- No vehicle
- No nearby services
- Odor concerns
- Pest concerns
- Storage limits
- Landlord restrictions
- Old or inefficient appliances

Do not treat a constraint as an excuse to dismiss. Constraints define what a useful solution must respect.

## Existing and Failed Solutions

Record what the person already tried, including:

- Products
- Routines
- DIY methods
- Storage systems
- Apps or reminders
- Advice from others
- Avoidance or postponement
- Workarounds

A failed solution is valuable only when the evidence explains why it failed.

Possible failure reasons include:

- Too expensive
- Too large
- Too many steps
- Hard to clean
- Easy to forget
- Created odor or mess
- Violated rental rules
- Required unavailable services
- Solved the wrong problem
- Added visual clutter
- Did not fit the routine

Do not call a solution failed merely because the person stopped using it without context.

## Evidence Strength

### Strong

The record is first-hand, specific, relevant, contextual, and clearly supports the classification.

### Moderate

The record is relevant and useful but lacks some context, specificity, or directness.

### Weak

The record hints at a theme but is generic, ambiguous, second-hand, or incomplete.

### Excluded

The record should not count toward findings because it is promotional, duplicated, unsupported, irrelevant, fabricated, too generic, or otherwise unreliable.

Evidence strength applies to the record's usefulness for the specific research question. A credible source can still be weak evidence for an unrelated claim.

## Exclusion Flags

Use one or more explicit flags:

- `generic`
- `promotional`
- `duplicate`
- `near_duplicate`
- `unsupported`
- `second_hand`
- `unclear_context`
- `wrong_audience`
- `wrong_problem`
- `solution_only`
- `emotion_unclear`
- `possible_incentive`
- `source_unavailable`
- `translation_risk`

Keep excluded records when useful for auditability, but remove them from theme counts.

## Duplicate Handling

Duplicates can falsely inflate a theme.

Check for:

- Exact duplicate quotes
- The same review syndicated to several retailers
- Screenshots or reposts of one source
- Near-identical comments from the same user
- One thread quoted in several summaries
- The same story repeated by the same person

Assign one canonical record and link duplicates to it.

Do not count multiple excerpts from one source as multiple independent people unless clearly justified.

## Theme Synthesis

After record review, synthesize only from included evidence.

A standard ETLH synthesis may report:

- Top recurring problems
- Strongest emotional signals
- Most common constraints
- Existing or failed solutions
- Jobs-to-be-done
- Underserved sub-segments
- Contradictory evidence
- Weak or absent themes
- Questions requiring more research

For each theme, include:

- Number of supporting records
- Evidence-strength mix
- Source diversity
- Representative examples
- Important limitations

Do not rank themes by impression alone when record counts are available.

## Recurrence and Prevalence Language

Use wording that matches the sample.

Appropriate:

- “This theme appeared in 12 of the 40 included records.”
- “Several renters in this sample described...”
- “The strongest recurring theme in the reviewed comments was...”

Avoid without representative data:

- “Most renters struggle with...”
- “Everyone wants...”
- “This is a widespread problem...”
- “The market is demanding...”

Qualitative recurrence does not establish population prevalence.

## Underserved Sub-Segment Standard

An underserved sub-segment should be supported by a recognizable combination of:

- Specific audience
- Specific situation
- Specific constraint
- Unmet job-to-be-done
- Weakness in current solutions

Do not invent a niche merely because it sounds marketable.

A sub-segment is stronger when several independent records show the same pattern and existing solutions repeatedly fail for the same reason.

## Contradictory Evidence

Record evidence that weakens the leading interpretation.

Examples:

- People who do not experience the problem
- Existing solutions that work well
- Different priorities within the audience
- Price sensitivity that conflicts with a product idea
- Readers who prefer convenience over waste reduction

Do not remove contradictory records to create a cleaner story.

## Statistics and Technical Sources

For statistics, regulations, health, safety, environmental impact, product performance, and technical claims, prefer primary or authoritative sources such as:

- Government agencies
- Original research papers
- Standards bodies
- Utility or municipal documentation
- Product manuals and technical specifications
- Direct company disclosures for product-specific facts

Record:

- Source title
- Publisher
- Publication date
- URL
- Method or sample
- Geography
- Relevant definitions
- Limitations
- Date last verified

Do not use an old statistic without checking whether it remains current.

## Product Research Standards

Product research should begin with the reader's job and constraints, not with available affiliate offers.

Evaluate:

- Exact problem solved
- Dimensions and fit for small spaces
- Upfront and recurring cost
- Required accessories
- Cleaning and maintenance
- Durability
- Rental compatibility
- Accessibility
- Return policy and warranty where relevant
- Independent complaints
- Whether a no-buy alternative exists

Separate:

- Manufacturer claims
- Retailer data
- Independent testing
- User reviews
- ETLH interpretation

Do not present review volume, ratings, popularity, or “viral” status as proof that a product is useful for ETLH readers.

## Trend and Market Research

When researching trends, distinguish:

- Search interest
- Social visibility
- Purchase intent
- Actual sales evidence
- Temporary novelty
- Durable reader need

A viral topic is not automatically an ETLH opportunity.

A market opportunity is stronger when:

- The problem appears repeatedly in first-hand evidence
- People are actively trying solutions
- Existing solutions have identifiable limitations
- The audience and constraints match ETLH
- The content can help even without a purchase

## Research Tools and AI Use

AI tools may assist with:

- Organizing records
- Suggesting tentative classifications
- Detecting possible duplicates
- Summarizing included evidence
- Formatting evidence tables
- Generating search variations

AI tools must not:

- Invent quotes or citations
- Fill missing source fields with guesses
- Convert a weak signal into a strong claim
- Treat generated summaries as source evidence
- Replace human review of the original context

Every material conclusion should remain traceable to source records.

## Research Review Workflow

### 1. Define

- State the research question or hypothesis.
- Define audience, scope, exclusions, and stopping condition.

### 2. Collect

- Gather traceable source records.
- Preserve exact wording and context.

### 3. Screen

- Remove irrelevant, promotional, duplicate, or unusable records from counting.
- Preserve exclusion reasons.

### 4. Classify

- Confirm or correct problem category.
- Confirm or correct dominant emotion.
- Extract job-to-be-done.
- Record constraints and attempted solutions.
- Assign evidence strength.

### 5. Challenge

- Look for contradictory evidence.
- Test whether the interpretation is stronger than the records allow.
- Identify missing audiences or sources.

### 6. Synthesize

- Count included records.
- Rank recurring themes cautiously.
- State limitations and uncertainty.

### 7. Translate

- Convert supported findings into content questions, briefs, product criteria, or further research needs.
- Do not jump directly from one quote to a broad strategy.

### 8. Archive

- Save raw records, cleaned records, synthesis, date, scope, and decision outcome.
- Preserve a path back to the original sources.

## Research-to-Content Handoff

A content brief based on research should include:

- Supported reader problem
- Audience and situation
- Job-to-be-done
- Key constraints
- Emotional context
- Existing or failed solutions
- Evidence summary
- Claims permitted by the evidence
- Claims not supported
- Practical action the content can teach
- Relevant source links
- Open questions

The brief should not include a stronger promise than the evidence supports.

## Research Quality Checklist

Before approving a synthesis, confirm:

### Scope

- Is the question clear?
- Are audience and exclusions defined?
- Is the sample described honestly?

### Source Integrity

- Can each material record be traced?
- Are exact quotes preserved?
- Is context sufficient?
- Are private or sensitive details handled responsibly?

### Classification

- Were categories confirmed rather than accepted automatically?
- Is the emotional signal supported?
- Is the job-to-be-done distinct from the solution?
- Are constraints recorded?

### Evidence Quality

- Are generic and promotional records excluded?
- Are duplicates controlled?
- Is evidence strength assigned consistently?
- Are contradictory records included?

### Synthesis

- Do counts use included records only?
- Does wording match the sample?
- Are weak themes labeled weak?
- Are limitations visible?
- Is the underserved segment supported rather than invented?

### Handoff

- Can editorial staff see what claims are allowed?
- Can every major conclusion be traced to evidence?
- Are unanswered questions preserved?

## Research Failure Conditions

Stop and correct the work when:

- Quotes cannot be traced
- Source context changes the interpretation
- Duplicate records materially inflate a theme
- The sample does not match the stated audience
- Promotional material dominates the findings
- AI-generated text has entered the evidence set
- A conclusion relies mainly on weak records
- The hypothesis is being defended instead of tested
- The proposed claim exceeds the evidence
