# ETLH Design System

## Purpose

Provide one durable visual reference for Eco Tiny Living Hub across the website, Instagram, Pinterest, and future downloadable resources.

The system should make ETLH recognizable while keeping every asset calm, practical, reader-centered, and appropriate for real small-space living.

## Scope

This document governs:

- Website visual styling
- Instagram carousel backgrounds and finished graphics
- Pinterest graphics
- Social-media typography and footer branding
- Photography and generated-background direction
- Layout, spacing, contrast, and text-safe composition
- Visual quality-control standards

It does not replace the Editorial Manual, Research Manual, or platform-specific publishing checklists.

## Status

Active canonical design reference.

This document consolidates the visual rules already established in the ETLH website styles and published Instagram production files. It does not introduce a new visual strategy.

## Core Visual Character

ETLH should feel:

- Calm
- Warm
- Practical
- Trustworthy
- Lived-in
- Editorial
- Peaceful
- Approachable
- Small-space aware
- Sustainable without looking performative

The visual system should communicate that meaningful change is reachable through small, repeatable actions. It should not communicate perfection, luxury, guilt, pressure, or an unrealistic showroom lifestyle.

## Visual Principles

### Real life over performance

Use environments and objects that look genuinely usable. Rooms may be clean and intentional, but they should not feel staged like an advertisement.

### Calm without sterility

Spaces should feel orderly enough to reduce visual friction while still showing normal signs of life: a mug, notebook, folded cloth, keys, reusable bag, mail, plant, or other practical objects.

### Progress over perfection

Do not portray sustainability as an all-or-nothing aesthetic. Avoid imagery that implies the reader needs a perfect home, expensive renovation, or complete lifestyle overhaul.

### Teach, do not preach

Visuals should support a useful idea or action. They should not rely on guilt, fear, moral superiority, or environmental catastrophe imagery.

### Reader-centered composition

The image exists to help the reader understand and use the message. Decorative choices should never compete with readability or the practical takeaway.

### Consistency with variety

Preserve the ETLH visual language while varying room zone, surface type, camera angle, crop, distance, object placement, and text-safe area. Do not repeatedly reuse one base apartment composition.

### Social visual palette

Instagram and Pinterest visuals should use the same overall family rather than forcing exact website hex values into every photograph:

- Warm cream walls and backgrounds
- Sage or moss green accents
- Natural wood tones
- Soft earth neutrals
- Warm whites
- Muted textiles
- Occasional clay or sand details

Avoid:

- Neon colors
- Cold blue-gray interiors
- High-saturation filters
- Stark black-and-white treatment
- Trend-driven palettes that overpower the ETLH identity
- Greenwashing clichés that use excessive bright green simply to signal “eco”

## Typography

### Website typography

The website currently uses:

- **Headlines:** Instrument Serif
- **Body and interface text:** Plus Jakarta Sans

Website headings use a restrained editorial serif treatment with slightly tightened spacing. Body copy should remain clean, highly readable, and practical.

### Instagram and Pinterest typography

The established social-graphic direction is:

- **Headlines:** Lora
- **Body copy:** Poppins

Use the social pair consistently within a carousel or graphic set. Do not mix additional decorative fonts into the same asset.

### Type hierarchy

Use a clear hierarchy:

1. Main headline
2. Supporting statement or explanation
3. Optional action label such as “Your Eco Step”
4. Small footer brand mark

Headline text should carry the main idea at a glance. Supporting text should be concise enough to read comfortably on a phone.

### Typography standards

- Use sentence case unless a specific small label is intentionally uppercase.
- Keep line lengths short on social graphics.
- Avoid overly thin weights over photography.
- Avoid large blocks of centered body text.
- Do not use script fonts, novelty fonts, faux-handwritten fonts, or aggressive condensed display fonts.
- Use enough contrast that text remains readable without zooming.

## Photography and Background Style

### Required visual feel

Use real-life lifestyle photography or realistic generated imagery with:

- Warm natural light
- Cream walls or warm cream tones
- Sage green accents
- Natural wood
- Small apartment proportions
- Clean but lived-in styling
- Peaceful, editorial composition
- Practical household objects
- Low-pressure, believable routines

### Suitable room zones

Vary scenes across:

- Entryway
- Kitchen counter
- Dining table
- Desk
- Nightstand
- Living room corner
- Bedroom corner
- Pantry or shelf
- Small storage zone
- Doorway station

### Suitable objects

Use objects that support the lesson, such as:

- Keys and a ceramic bowl
- Reusable tote bag
- Reusable bottle
- Mug
- Notebook and pen
- Folded cloth
- Mail or papers
- Basket or tray
- Hooks
- Shoes near an entryway
- Houseplants
- Simple storage containers

Objects should appear for a reason. Avoid filling a scene with generic decor that does not support the message.

### Human presence

Hands or partial human action may be shown when useful, such as:

- Placing keys in a bowl
- Clearing one item from a surface
- Hanging a tote bag
- Grouping items into a tray
- Writing in a notebook

The action should feel candid and practical, not posed like a commercial.

### Prohibited visual treatments

Do not use:

- Luxury homes that conflict with the renter and budget audience
- Oversized kitchens or unrealistic storage rooms as the default setting
- Perfectly sterile rooms
- Heavy clutter used for shock value
- Guilt-based before-and-after imagery
- Product-ad styling
- Fake badges, ratings, labels, or environmental claims
- Readable brand names or packaging unless specifically approved
- Text, letters, logos, signs, labels, or readable writing inside generated background images

When notebooks, sticky notes, calendars, labels, or signs appear in a background image, they must be blank or unreadable.

## Social Image Formats

### Instagram carousel

Default production format:

- 1080 × 1350 px
- Vertical 4:5
- Five slides unless the content brief specifies otherwise
- Each slide exported as a separate image file

Do not deliver a five-slide carousel as one combined image.

### Pinterest

Pinterest graphics should be vertically optimized unless another size is explicitly requested.

When a square Pinterest variant is requested, use the stated dimensions, including 600 × 600 px where specified.

### Background-only workflow

Visual prompts and copy templates are separate production steps.

Unless the brief explicitly requests a finished graphic, image-generation prompts should request background images only, with no added text, logo, footer, labels, or typography.

## Composition and Text-Safe Space

Every social background intended for later text overlay must include large, intentional negative space.

### Text-safe standards

- Reserve enough uninterrupted space for both headline and supporting copy.
- Keep the text-safe zone visually quiet.
- Avoid placing high-contrast objects, faces, plant leaves, window frames, or clutter behind the planned copy.
- Use natural wall space, tabletop space, soft bedding, floor area, or shallow-focus background as the text-safe region.
- Confirm the safe area is large enough before approving the image.

### Variation requirement

Do not place the text-safe area in the same location on every slide.

Vary it among:

- Upper left
- Upper right
- Left side
- Right side
- Upper third
- Lower third when readability permits

The focal object may sit opposite the text-safe region, such as on the right or lower-right when copy is planned for the left.

### Carousel visual rhythm

A five-slide carousel should feel connected but not repetitive. A useful sequence may include:

1. Establishing room scene
2. Friction or problem detail
3. Practical system or object close-up
4. Human-action scene
5. Calm completed state

This is a composition pattern, not a mandatory story formula.

## Social Graphic Overlay System

### Headline

Use Lora with strong mobile readability. Keep the headline concise and allow comfortable line spacing.

### Body copy

Use Poppins. Keep supporting copy short, plainspoken, and easy to scan.

### Divider or accent

A restrained sage or moss divider may be used to organize the copy. It should support hierarchy rather than decorate the slide unnecessarily.

### Footer brand mark

Use the subtle ETLH footer treatment established in Instagram 005.

Beginning with Instagram 005, the footer treatment should use the slightly darker, higher-contrast version created for mobile readability.

The footer should remain secondary to the lesson but must be legible at normal phone size.

Do not enlarge the footer into a dominant logo banner.

## Logo Use

Concept A is the approved primary ETLH logo direction.

Use the approved logo or simplified ETLH footer mark only when the production brief calls for branding. Do not insert a logo into background-only image generations.

Logo use should be:

- Clear
- Unstretched
- Uncropped
- High enough contrast to remain legible
- Surrounded by adequate breathing room
- Secondary to the reader-facing message

Do not recreate, redraw, or modify the logo from memory when the approved source asset is unavailable.

## Website Interface Standards

The website should preserve the current warm editorial system:

- Cream primary background
- Dark earth text
- Moss accents
- White cards
- Soft low-opacity borders
- Rounded corners
- Generous spacing
- Serif headings
- Sans-serif body copy

### Corners and surfaces

The source system uses a base radius of `0.75rem`, with larger rounded treatments for cards, images, fields, and feature sections.

### Contrast

Primary text should use Earth 900 or an equivalent high-contrast treatment. Earth 500 is appropriate for supporting copy only when readability remains strong.

### Buttons and links

- Moss is the primary action color.
- Earth 900 may be used for strong secondary actions.
- Hover states should be visible but restrained.
- Avoid decorative button styles that conflict with the calm editorial character.

## Accessibility Standards

- Text must remain readable at normal mobile size.
- Do not rely on color alone to communicate meaning.
- Maintain strong text-to-background contrast.
- Avoid placing copy directly over visually busy photography.
- Every published carousel package must include alt text for every slide.
- Alt text should describe both the meaningful visual scene and the essential overlaid message.
- Decorative details do not need exhaustive description.
- Do not embed important information only inside an image when a text equivalent is available elsewhere.

## Production Workflow

For Instagram content, use the locked sequence:

1. Visual Batch Prompt
2. Copy Template
3. Caption + Hashtags in one copy box
4. First Comment
5. Alt Text
6. Codex Backlog Addition when needed

### Visual production checks

Before accepting a background batch, confirm:

- Correct dimensions and orientation
- Separate file for each slide
- No accidental text or readable labels
- Adequate text-safe negative space
- Varied camera angle and room zone
- Warm natural lighting
- Cream, sage, and natural-wood visual language
- Realistic small-space scale
- Clean but lived-in styling
- No ad-like staging

### Final carousel checks

Before publishing, confirm:

- Headline and body hierarchy are clear
- Copy is readable on a phone
- Footer mark uses the established higher-contrast treatment
- Slides feel visually related but not duplicated
- Caption and hashtags are finalized together
- First comment is finalized
- Alt text is finalized for every slide
- Final assets are archived in the appropriate post folder when the workflow calls for repository archiving

## Quality-Control Test

A finished ETLH visual should pass these questions:

1. Does it feel calm, practical, and believable?
2. Does it look appropriate for a renter or small-apartment reader?
3. Is the useful idea clearer than the decoration?
4. Is the text readable at normal phone size?
5. Does the image avoid guilt, perfectionism, and ad-like staging?
6. Does it preserve ETLH consistency without repeating the same base shot?
7. Does it support one small, concrete, repeatable action or insight?
8. Would the reader leave with more clarity rather than more pressure?

If the answer to any core question is no, revise the asset before publishing.
