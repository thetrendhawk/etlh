# ETLH Site Asset Production Standard

## Purpose

Define a durable production, review, storage, and delivery standard for ETLH website, social, and downloadable assets. The goal is useful reader support, truthful presentation, accessible delivery, and consistent visual quality—not decorative volume.

This standard does not authorize a topic cluster, article, image, download, account change, or publication. Every asset still requires an approved destination and the normal review workflow.

## Core principles

1. **Useful before decorative.** An asset should explain, orient, compare, guide, or reduce reader effort.
2. **Illustration is not evidence.** Generated, staged, stock, or illustrative scenes cannot prove a transformation, cost, saving, result, testimonial, or lived experience.
3. **One role per deliverable.** Website hero, article card, Open Graph image, Pinterest pin, Instagram post, and worksheet are separate delivery roles even when they share a visual source.
4. **Accessible meaning lives outside pixels.** Important instructions, claims, tables, and checklists must have an HTML or document text equivalent.
5. **Provenance travels with the asset.** Source, creator/tool, license, permissions, edits, approval, and intended uses must remain traceable.
6. **Mobile is a production requirement.** Every crop, overlay, label, and text size must be checked at its smallest intended display.
7. **No perfection theater.** ETLH visuals should feel calm and considered without implying that sustainable living requires a flawless home, expensive purchase, or complete makeover.

## Approved visual roles

### Lifestyle illustration

Use to establish place, mood, or a plausible everyday situation.

- Suitable for website heroes, category orientation, article cards, and decorative support.
- Must be labeled as illustrative when a reasonable reader might interpret it as ETLH's real home, a contributor's experience, or a documented result.
- Must not depict a fabricated before/after pair, branded endorsement, exact price outcome, or measurable result.
- Prefer one concrete, article-relevant action over generic collections of “eco” objects.

### Instructional diagram

Use to explain placement, sequence, dimensions, zones, flow, or a system.

- Examples: pantry rotation zones, room circulation paths, food-use flow, or a renter-safe setup sequence.
- Labels must be legible, concise, and repeated in adjacent HTML or document text.
- Measurements must state units, assumptions, and whether they are examples rather than universal requirements.
- Diagrams must not imply building-code, food-safety, health, or engineering authority without appropriate review.

### Checklist graphic

Use as a concise visual reminder, not as the only accessible checklist.

- Keep the complete checklist in HTML, an accessible document, or both.
- Avoid universal commands where suitability varies by home, budget, local rules, accessibility needs, household, or renter agreement.
- Do not embed claims such as “save money,” “better planet,” or “healthier home” without evidence and conditions.

### Comparison graphic

Use to compare documented criteria, options, or tradeoffs—not to manufacture a winner.

- Name the comparison basis and keep units consistent.
- Disclose missing, variable, or location-dependent information.
- “Before and after” is permitted only for a documented sequence with provenance, dates, matching context, and a truthful explanation of what changed.
- Never use illustrative or generated halves as documentary transformation evidence.

### Worksheet

Use when the reader must record decisions, measurements, observations, or next steps.

- Provide adequate writing space and logical tab order.
- Separate prompts from examples.
- Do not require an email address to understand what the worksheet contains.
- A mobile-friendly HTML or fillable alternative is preferred when the worksheet is central to the article's value.

### Documentary evidence

Use only for original or licensed photographs, scans, receipts, measurements, screenshots, records, or sequences that substantiate a factual statement.

- Preserve original files and metadata where lawful and appropriate.
- Record who created the evidence, when, where, under what conditions, and what edits were made.
- Redact personal, account, payment, location, or bystander information before publication.
- Captions must explain what the evidence does and does not establish.
- Material cropping, cleanup, compositing, generative fill, object removal, or scene alteration must be disclosed and may disqualify the asset as documentary evidence.

### Decorative support image

Use to add rhythm or atmosphere when it does not convey required information.

- Use empty alt text in HTML when adjacent text already supplies all meaning.
- Do not place decorative assets where they interrupt the reading task or create avoidable transfer cost.
- Decorative assets do not need to be added to every section or article.

## ETLH visual direction

### Preserve

- realistic small-space lifestyle imagery;
- warm natural light;
- cream or warm-white walls;
- sage green accents;
- natural wood;
- calm small apartments;
- clean but lived-in rooms;
- editorial, practical framing;
- visible renter constraints and ordinary objects;
- progress without guilt or perfectionism.

### Vary deliberately

- room zones: kitchen counter, narrow pantry, entry, desk, living area, bedside, bathroom storage, laundry corner, balcony, and shared surfaces;
- camera angle: eye level, overhead only when instructional, close detail, doorway view, side view, and measured plan view;
- crop: wide environmental scene, medium action, close process detail, and negative-space composition;
- time and light: morning, overcast daylight, late afternoon, and practical evening task lighting;
- people and actions where permission and representation standards are met.

### Avoid

- showroom staging, luxury-renovation cues, and advertisement-like product arrays;
- rooms filled with matching new containers presented as the default sustainable solution;
- guilt-based clutter comparisons;
- impossible plant density or generic “green” symbolism as a substitute for a useful idea;
- fake handwriting, malformed labels, unreadable books, invented brands, or accidental logos;
- repeated beige-and-sage rooms from the same angle across unrelated articles;
- visual claims that a product, material, plant, or routine is universally safer, healthier, cheaper, or better for the planet.

## Truthful labeling rules

Choose one classification before production:

- **Documentary:** substantiates a real ETLH-relevant event, object, process, or result.
- **Instructional:** explains a system, sequence, measurement, or decision.
- **Illustrative:** represents a plausible situation but is not evidence that it occurred.
- **Decorative:** supplies atmosphere only.

The classification belongs in the asset record. Add a visible caption or nearby disclosure when the distinction is material to reader interpretation.

Use direct language:

- “Illustrative small-room layout” rather than “our transformation.”
- “Example placement; measure your space” rather than “the perfect layout.”
- “Possible lower-cost option” rather than “saves money.”
- “Example pantry zones” rather than “before and after” when no real sequence exists.

Do not use an asterisk or hidden metadata to cure a misleading headline inside an image.

## Generated-image limitations

Generated imagery may be used only as **illustrative** or **decorative** material after visual, provenance, and rights review.

Generated imagery must not:

- represent a real reader, contributor, expert, home, receipt, result, product test, before/after, or case study;
- fabricate documentary evidence or imply firsthand ETLH experience;
- contain readable text, labels, prices, measurements, logos, certifications, receipts, screenshots, or product packaging;
- depict unsafe, impossible, or misleading household, food-storage, electrical, plant, accessibility, or renovation conditions;
- imitate a living artist, photographer, identifiable private person, or protected brand style without authorization;
- be published without recording the tool/model, generation date, prompt or brief reference, material edits, reviewer, and permitted uses.

Do not remove a generator watermark to make an asset appear photographic or independently sourced. A generated image with a visible third-party watermark is not publication-ready.

## Documentary-evidence requirements

Before an asset is labeled documentary, record:

- creator/photographer and rights holder;
- capture date and, where relevant, general location or environment;
- subject consent and property release status;
- original file identifier and preserved original hash;
- sequence relationship for before/after sets;
- matching camera position, meaningful time interval, and changed variables for comparisons;
- receipts, measurements, or source records supporting prices or quantities;
- edits, crops, redactions, and color adjustments;
- caption and claim supported;
- reviewer and approval date.

A before/after pair must not change lighting, staging, camera angle, crop, focal length, or removed items in ways that exaggerate the result without disclosure.

| Role/classification        | Documentary, instructional, illustrative, or decorative                               |
| Creator/tool               | Photographer, designer, owner, stock provider, or generation tool/model               |
| Creation/acquisition date  | ISO date                                                                              |
| Source URL/order/license   | Durable reference where applicable                                                    |
| Rights holder              | Person or organization                                                                |
| License and restrictions   | Commercial use, modification, attribution, territory, expiration, and platform limits |

## Alt-text rules

1. Write alt text for the asset's purpose in its exact context, not for search keywords.
2. Describe only visible, meaningful information. Do not infer that an object is thrifted, sustainable, zero waste, safe, healthy, affordable, or effective from appearance.
3. Use `alt=""` when a linked image is decorative and adjacent text already names the destination.
4. Do not start with “image of” unless the medium itself matters.
5. Do not copy an article title into alt text when it adds no visual information.
6. For a simple diagram, state the takeaway and provide labels in nearby text.
7. For a complex diagram, provide a concise alt plus an adjacent long description, table, or structured HTML equivalent.
8. For documentary evidence, identify the visible subject and relevant context without overstating what the image proves.
9. For a series, distinguish each frame rather than repeating one generic description.
10. Verify the final crop; alt text must not describe content removed by responsive cropping.

## Embedded-text rules

- Website lifestyle heroes and cards should contain no embedded text.
- Important information must exist as selectable, zoomable, screen-reader-accessible text.
- Social and worksheet text must be authored in the layout tool, not generated inside a background image.
- Keep statements short, conditional where necessary, and consistent with the approved destination.
- Do not embed URLs likely to change. Use the current canonical domain only when a visible URL adds user value.
- Do not embed prices, savings, guarantees, health outcomes, environmental outcomes, professional authority, or “before/after” language without completed evidence review.
- Avoid text over visually busy areas and never rely on a translucent panel that fails contrast at mobile size.
- Proofread exported pixels at 100% and at expected mobile display size.

## Responsive-image requirements

- Keep intrinsic width and height in markup to prevent layout shift.
- Provide `srcset` and accurate `sizes` for website imagery.
- Serve derivatives near the rendered size; do not send a 1600 px master to a 320 px card.
- Preserve the source URL as a fallback when required by the current Vercel image pipeline.
- Use eager/high priority only for the true initial above-the-fold image; lazy-load later assets.
- Verify the fallback, 320/390 px mobile, tablet, desktop, high-density, and social-crawler outputs.
- Check focal point, faces/hands, labels, and instructional arrows at every crop.
- Do not use CSS cropping to hide a watermark, unsupported claim, obsolete domain, or misleading part of an asset.

## File formats and compression

### WebP

Default for photographic or complex illustrative website assets when browser delivery supports it. Start near quality 72–82 and inspect edges, skin, hands, labels, and gradients.

### AVIF

Use for photographic assets when the build/delivery path supplies a reliable WebP/JPEG fallback and visual QA confirms no texture or color damage. Do not introduce AVIF merely for theoretical savings.

### JPEG

Acceptable for original photography, broad compatibility, and controlled fallback. Export in sRGB, strip unnecessary metadata from public derivatives, and preserve a separate original when metadata is evidence.

### PNG

Use for transparency, crisp limited-color graphics, or lossless intermediate work—not routine photographs. Optimize before commit.

### SVG

Preferred for icons, logos, and diagrams when content is trusted and sanitized. Keep text accessible or provide an equivalent. Do not embed unreviewed scripts, external references, or raster payloads.

### PDF

Use tagged, searchable, selectable text; logical reading order; document title and language; descriptive links; form labels where applicable; and print-safe contrast. Avoid image-only PDFs.

## Naming and folder structure

Use lowercase kebab-case and make the role explicit:

```text
src/assets/
  brand/
    etlh-mark.svg
  site/
    home/
      home-hero-small-apartment-v1.webp
    categories/
      zero-waste-kitchen-card-v1.webp
  articles/
    {article-slug}/
      {article-slug}-hero-v1.webp
      {article-slug}-card-v1.webp
      {article-slug}-diagram-food-rotation-v1.svg
  social/
    pinterest/
      {article-slug}-pin-{angle}-v1.webp
    instagram/
      {package-id}-slide-01-v1.webp
public/
  downloads/
    {resource-slug}/
      {resource-slug}-v1.pdf
assets/
  records/
    asset-manifest.md
  archive/
    README.md
```

Archive originals must not be imported into the application or copied into `public/`. If sensitive or large documentary originals should not live in Git, store them in an owner-approved controlled system and keep only the record/reference in the repository.

Avoid names such as `image1`, `final-final`, `pin2`, `eco-photo`, or a claim that has not been verified. Do not overwrite an approved file while keeping the same version identifier.

## Versioning and archive rules

- Increment `v1`, `v2`, and so on for material visual, text, crop, or claim changes.
- Compression-only derivatives may use the same content version plus a role/size suffix.
- Record the superseding asset and reason before withdrawing a public asset.
- Remove obsolete assets from the application source graph; Git history is not a public asset archive strategy.
- Never force-push or rewrite published repository history.
- Archive does not mean approved for reuse. Mark status and restrictions explicitly.
- Retain documentary originals according to owner-approved privacy and retention rules; do not invent a retention period.

## Mobile crop and negative-space checks

For every lifestyle or documentary master:

- test at 320, 390, 768, 1024, and the largest intended container;
- check `object-cover` crops and social-platform previews;
- confirm no face, hand, tool, label, measurement, or essential action is cut off;
- confirm the crop does not change documentary meaning;
- verify at least one alternate crop when the same source supports multiple roles;
- reject a source that only works after extreme cropping.

For later text-overlay production, reserve intentional negative space during the source brief:

- at least 35–45% calm visual area for a short social headline;
- consistent tonal area without faces, hands, labels, windows, or high-frequency detail;
- text-safe area identified for the exact role, not assumed from a different aspect ratio;
- separate clean background master and finished text-overlay export.

## Production workflow

1. Confirm the live or approved destination and reader task.
2. Choose the visual role and evidence classification.
3. Write the asset brief, including claims boundary and required text equivalent.
4. Resolve source, license, release, privacy, and generation limitations before production.
5. Produce the role-specific master and variants.
6. Complete truthfulness, accessibility, crop, contrast, spelling, domain, and brand review.
7. Optimize and record dimensions, size, checksum, version, and permitted uses.
8. Add only approved web-ready sources to the application path.
9. Run formatting, asset, content, build, accessibility, responsive, metadata, and exact-head preview checks.
10. Record approval and later replacement/withdrawal history.

## Definition of done

An asset is production-ready only when:

- its destination, role, and reader benefit are clear;
- its documentary/instructional/illustrative/decorative classification is recorded;
- provenance, license, generation status, releases, edits, and permitted uses are complete;
- every visible and implied claim is supported or removed;
- alt, caption, long description, or decorative treatment is approved;
- embedded text is accurate, readable, and available in an accessible equivalent;
- role-specific size, crop, negative space, contrast, and mobile checks pass;
- optimized files meet current budgets without material degradation;
- no retired domain, obsolete logo, watermark, accidental label, or invented brand remains;
- repository CI and exact-head preview gates pass;
- the owner approves any documentary evidence, paid/license obligation, new public download, email gate, or brand-master change.
