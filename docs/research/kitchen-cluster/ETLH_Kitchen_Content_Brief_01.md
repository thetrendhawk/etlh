## 1. Reader problem

> A renter living in a compact apartment wants to collect food scraps for an available organics destination but is constrained by odor, fruit flies, leaks, limited freezer/counter space, and the cadence of moving scraps out of the apartment.

- **Desired outcome:** choose a workable staging method and diagnose a scrap container that already smells or attracts flies
- **Observed consequence:** disgust, anxiety, repeated emptying, and abandonment risk

## 4. Article promise and scope

> After reading, a renter will be able to identify the likely source of odor or fruit flies, clean and reset the staging container safely, and select a storage/removal routine that fits the space and an actually available organics destination.

### Included

- first-response checklist for odor, leaks, residue, and fruit flies
- counter versus freezer versus sealed-fermentation staging tradeoffs
- destination-first planning: accepted materials, storage time, and removal cadence
- no-dishwasher cleaning path
- renter-aware placement, shared-space, and container-size questions

### Explicit exclusions

- state-by-state or national tenant-rights advice
- claims about rules outside the program explicitly named and sourced
- “zero smell,” “odorless,” or pest-free guarantees
- manufacturer-performance conclusions based on Reddit or Trustpilot
- quantified capacity recommendations without measured assumptions
- standalone roommate or health-condition advice based on the anecdotal records

## 5. Differentiation and claims guardrails

> Existing results commonly teach how to start. ETLH will add value with a symptom-first sequence, explicit small-space tradeoffs, a no-dishwasher path, and visible evidence boundaries for renters whose scraps already smell or attract flies.

Claims not permitted:

- a method produces no smell in all homes
- freezer storage is maintenance-free or universally available
- bokashi output is finished compost without qualification
- a community garden or farmers' market accepts scraps without checking

## 7. Recommended article structure

### Introduction

- Start with the already-smelly-bin scenario.
- State that some odor risk is manageable but no setup is guaranteed odor-free.
- Ask the reader to confirm where the scraps will ultimately go.

### H2: Stop the immediate odor or fruit-fly problem

- isolate leaks and residue; empty according to local acceptance rules
- follow container care and authoritative cleanup instructions
- use a symptom → likely cause → next check table

### H2: Why a small scrap container starts smelling

- time, temperature, moisture, residue, lid/seal, and accepted contents
- separate sourced technical explanations from reader anecdotes

### H2: Choose a staging method that fits the apartment

- counter container, freezer-safe container, or sealed fermentation system
- compare space, cadence, upkeep, destination, and failure modes
- do not label a universal winner

### H2: Build the removal routine around a real destination

- verify accepted materials, drop-off days, access, and container rules
- include a fallback when a preferred destination is unavailable

### H2: Clean the container with or without a dishwasher

- separate manufacturer-specific care from general workflow
- include hand-washing and drying space constraints

### H2: When the setup still does not work

- shorten staging time, change placement/container, or choose a different accepted route
- avoid recommending purchases before diagnosing the failure

### Conclusion

- choose one symptom fix and one removal-cadence change
- link to the pantry and chef-habits pages only after their claim repairs are live
